Subtheme: Corporate Landlords and Evictions

This analysis examines how ownership structure shapes Boston’s housing market, with a particular focus on the distinction between owner-occupied housing, absentee ownership, and corporate landlords. Prior research suggests that corporate and non-local landlords are more likely to file evictions, while owner-occupants or locally based landlords may have greater flexibility in responding to missed or late rent payments. These differences are frequently discussed in relation to housing stability, displacement, and eviction risk.

Background readings for this subtheme, including In 83 Million Eviction Records, a Sweeping and Intimate New Look at Housing in America (New York Times) and the Homes for All MA report on eviction filings during COVID, frame eviction not simply as a legal event, but as an outcome shaped by market structure, ownership incentives, and broader socioeconomic conditions. Rather than focusing on individual eviction cases, this work emphasizes patterns at scale and asks whether certain communities are systematically more exposed to eviction risk.

Using longitudinal, neighborhood-level data on corporate ownership rates and owner-occupancy from 2004 to 2024, this exploratory analysis investigates how ownership patterns in Boston have changed over time, where those changes are most pronounced, and how corporate ownership and owner-occupancy relate to neighborhood demographics. The goal is not to make causal claims about eviction behavior, but to assess whether the ownership patterns described in prior research are visible in Boston’s housing data and to surface spatial and temporal trends that merit deeper investigation.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How have corporate ownership rates and owner-occupancy rates changed in Boston neighborhoods from 2004 to 2024?

    Motivation: Prior work on evictions and housing stability often points to ownership structure as an important factor, particularly the distinction between owner-occupied housing and properties held by corporate or absentee landlords. Before evaluating any downstream outcomes such as eviction risk, it is necessary to understand how ownership patterns themselves have evolved over time. This question is motivated by claims in both the New York Times eviction analysis and the Homes for All MA report that corporate and absentee ownership has increased in recent decades, potentially changing the structure of the rental market.

  2. Which neighborhoods experienced the largest increases in corporate ownership or decreases in owner-occupancy, and what do the extremes look like in 2024?

    Motivation: Aggregate citywide trends can obscure meaningful variation at the neighborhood level. Prior research emphasizes that the impacts of housing market changes are not evenly distributed across space, and that some communities may be more exposed to investor activity than others. Identifying neighborhoods at the extremes of these trends helps connect the data to ongoing policy discussions about where housing pressures are most concentrated and where interventions may be most relevant.

  3. Are neighborhoods with higher corporate ownership also characterized by lower owner-occupancy, and do these patterns differ across neighborhood demographics?

    Motivation: The background readings suggest a relationship between corporate or absentee ownership and housing instability, with particular attention to racial and socioeconomic disparities in eviction filings. While this dataset does not include eviction outcomes directly, examining the relationship between corporate ownership, owner-occupancy, and neighborhood demographic composition provides a way to assess whether the structural patterns discussed in prior work appear in Boston’s housing data. This question serves as a pattern-check rather than a causal test, helping to evaluate whether ownership structure and demographics are spatially correlated.

Data Notes, Assumptions & Transformations

Dataset structure

  • Unit of analysis: Each row represents a Boston neighborhood in a given year (2004–2024).
  • Key measures: corp_own_rate (corporate ownership rate) and own_occ_rate (owner-occupancy rate), reported as proportions (0–1).
  • Demographic context: Neighborhood-level counts by race/ethnicity (e.g., white_all, black_all, hisp_all, aapi_all) and population totals (e.g., tot_pop_all).

Quality checks

  • Range checks: Rate variables were checked to ensure they fall within [0, 1].
  • Missing values: Rows with missing values in required fields were excluded only for figures that required them (e.g., the 2024 scatter requires corp_own_rate, own_occ_rate, and demographic context).
  • Population denominator: Demographic shares were computed using tot_pop_all. If tot_pop_all equals zero, shares are treated as missing to avoid division-by-zero artifacts.

Interpretation assumptions

  • Owner-occupancy as a proxy: Lower own_occ_rate is treated as a rough proxy for higher non-owner occupancy and potentially more absentee ownership, but it does not directly identify landlord location or management practices.
  • Corporate ownership: corp_own_rate is interpreted as the share of properties held by corporate entities; this may include small LLCs or corporate forms used by local owners.
  • Demographic correlations are not causal: Demographics are used to provide context for where ownership patterns cluster and are not interpreted as causes of ownership change or eviction risk.

Transformations and analytical choices

  • Demographic shares: Created derived variables such as pct_nonwhite = 1 - white_all / tot_pop_all.
  • Weighted averages over time: Computed occupied-unit–weighted averages by year using occ_unit.
  • Change over time: Computed neighborhood-level deltas from 2004 to 2024 (e.g., corp_own_rate_2024 − corp_own_rate_2004).
  • Association measures: Used Pearson correlation to summarize the linear relationship between corporate ownership and owner-occupancy across neighborhoods each year.
  • Selective labeling rule: Labels in the 2024 scatter are applied using predefined rules (top corporate ownership, lowest owner-occupancy, highest non-white share) rather than hand-picked examples.

Scope note: This dataset does not include eviction filings directly, so this analysis focuses on ownership structure patterns that prior work links to eviction risk, rather than measuring eviction outcomes themselves.

Discoveries & Insights

Synthesizing Findings

Q1: How have corporate ownership and owner-occupancy changed over time?

Taken together, Figures 1 and 2 show a broad and sustained shift in Boston’s neighborhood ownership structure from 2004 to 2024. Distributional changes indicate that increases in corporate ownership are not confined to a small subset of neighborhoods, while weighted trends demonstrate that these changes persist over time and accelerate in later years. The parallel decline in owner-occupancy suggests a citywide restructuring rather than short-term fluctuation, supporting claims in prior literature that ownership patterns have meaningfully changed over the past two decades.

Q2: Which neighborhoods experienced the largest changes?

Figure 3 demonstrates that while ownership shifts are widespread, the magnitude of change varies substantially across neighborhoods. Some areas experienced especially large increases in corporate ownership or sharp declines in owner-occupancy, while others remained relatively stable. The fact that these two sets of neighborhoods do not perfectly overlap suggests that changes in owner-occupancy are not driven by a single mechanism. This finding reinforces the importance of neighborhood-level analysis and cautions against relying solely on citywide averages when evaluating housing market dynamics.

Q3: How do ownership patterns relate to demographics?

Figures 4 and 5 together indicate a stable inverse relationship between corporate ownership and owner-occupancy that persists across time and neighborhoods. When demographic context is introduced, Figure 4 highlights that neighborhoods with lower owner-occupancy and higher corporate ownership often also have higher non-white population shares. While this pattern does not imply causality, it aligns with prior research suggesting that ownership structure and housing instability may be unevenly distributed across communities. These results motivate deeper investigation into how ownership patterns, demographics, and eviction-related outcomes intersect.

Follow-up Questions & Next Steps

  • Linking ownership structure to eviction outcomes: This dataset does not include eviction filings directly. A natural next step would merge neighborhood-level ownership patterns with eviction filing data to evaluate whether areas with higher corporate ownership or lower owner-occupancy experience higher filing rates, as suggested by prior research.
  • Distinguishing long-standing patterns from recent change: Some neighborhoods exhibit high corporate ownership in 2024 but relatively small changes since 2004, while others show rapid recent increases. Separating level effects from growth dynamics could help distinguish historically investor-dominated areas from neighborhoods undergoing newer transitions.
  • Disaggregating “corporate” ownership: The corporate ownership measure includes a range of entity types, from large institutional investors to small LLCs. Further work could attempt to differentiate these categories to assess whether ownership concentration or scale matters for housing stability.
  • Housing stock and redevelopment effects: Changes in owner-occupancy may reflect redevelopment, condo conversions, or changes in housing type rather than corporate acquisition alone. Incorporating housing age, unit type, or redevelopment indicators could clarify these mechanisms.
  • Policy and temporal breakpoints: The acceleration of corporate ownership in later years raises questions about whether specific policy changes, market shocks, or post-2008 dynamics influenced ownership trends. Identifying breakpoints could contextualize these shifts.

Summary

From 2004 to 2024, Boston neighborhoods experienced a substantial increase in corporate ownership alongside a general decline in owner-occupancy. Distributional and temporal analyses suggest that this shift is broad-based rather than driven by a small number of extreme neighborhoods, indicating a citywide restructuring of ownership patterns. At the same time, the largest changes are concentrated in specific neighborhoods, highlighting the importance of pairing aggregate trends with neighborhood-level views to avoid obscuring localized dynamics.

Across the full time period, corporate ownership and owner-occupancy are strongly and consistently inversely related, suggesting a stable structural relationship rather than a short-term or one-off effect. This supports the use of owner-occupancy as a reasonable proxy for absentee or non-local ownership in exploratory analysis. Taken together, these findings align with prior research on housing stability and motivate deeper follow-on questions about how changes in ownership structure intersect with neighborhood demographics, housing stock characteristics, and eviction-related outcomes.
